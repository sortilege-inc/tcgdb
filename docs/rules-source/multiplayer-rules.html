Part A — Enlightenment Format (3 players)

A three-player head-to-head format introducing the Enlightenment victory condition. Additional players can be added by altering the draw-phase rules (see p. 3). The standard game rules apply wherever not explicitly altered, replaced, or negated here.

Enlightenment — Overview & Setup

Overview

The Enlightenment format is a way to play L5R LCG with three players, introducing the Enlightenment victory condition (win by collecting all five elemental rings on your provinces).

Setup

Follow standard setup (RRG p. 17) with this change to Step 4:

  • Players do not set aside rings to form a shared unclaimed ring pool. Instead, each player sets aside their own set of ring tokens into a personal unclaimed ring pool.
  • The general token pool is still created (fate, honor, status tokens, and the Imperial Favor card within reach of each player).
  • There is a common unclaimed ring pool available to all players, but it begins the game with no rings in it.

Enlightenment — Victory and Defeat

The standard win/lose rules are replaced. Three ways for a player to win an Enlightenment game:

  • Enlightenment: collect one of each elemental ring on their unbroken provinces;
  • Honorable Victory: accumulate 25 or more honor;
  • have each of their opponents eliminated.

Enlightenment (the ring victory)

  • A player collecting one copy of each elemental ring on their unbroken provinces immediately wins.
  • Only rings claimed on a player's province count toward the victory condition, and each counts only as a ring of its printed element. A ring counted as being in a player's claimed ring pool but not claimed on their provinces (via a card effect) does not count toward the victory condition.
  • A player can take rings claimed on opponents' provinces by breaking those provinces.
  • A player cannot have multiple rings of the same printed element claimed on their provinces.

Honorable Victory

A player with 25 or more honor in their honor pool immediately wins.

Player Elimination

  • A player whose stronghold is broken, or who has no honor, is immediately eliminated.
  • Any ring tokens an eliminated player had claimed on their provinces, or in their personal unclaimed ring pool, are placed in the common unclaimed ring pool.
  • A player's stronghold cannot be attacked unless all four of their non-stronghold provinces are broken.
  • When a player is eliminated:
    • Any cards owned by them are immediately removed from the game.
    • Any treaties they made are immediately dissolved.
    • Their honor dial is ignored for card-effect purposes for the rest of the game.
    • If they were the first player, the first player token passes to the player on their left.
  • Lasting effects from an eliminated player's cards persist for their normal duration; delayed effects still occur at their specified time.

Enlightenment — Honor Bids & the Draw Phase

Players bid honor with their dial to determine the number of cards drawn, as in the standard game, but the exchange of honor is handled differently with three players. The three-player draw phase has 3 steps:

  1. Each player secretly selects a number between 1 and 5 on their honor dial.
  2. Each player reveals their selection and players exchange honor (see Exchanging Honor).
  3. Each player draws cards from their conflict deck equal to the number selected.

Exchanging Honor

  • All three different: the player who selected the highest gives honor to the player who selected the lowest, equal to the difference between those two numbers. The middle player neither gains nor loses.
  • Two the same, third higher: the higher player gives to both opponents, an amount equal to the difference between the two numbers, divided as evenly as possible between the two tied players. Any leftover honor: the giver chooses which opponent gets it.
  • Two the same, third lower: the lower player takes from both opponents, total equal to the difference, divided as evenly as possible. Any leftover: the receiver chooses which opponent to take it from.
  • All three the same: no honor is exchanged.

The Draw Phase with Four or More Players

With 4+ players, honor bids during the draw phase work differently (the draw phase has 4 steps):

  1. Each player secretly selects either 2 or 4 on their honor dial.
  2. Each player reveals their selection. This value cannot be modified by card effects.
  3. Honor is exchanged: each player who selected 2 is acting "honorably" and gives away no honor; each player who selected 4 is acting "dishonorably" and gives 1 honor token to each player acting honorably.
  4. Each player draws cards equal to the number selected (2 or 4).

During duels, honor bids work as in the RRG, with all five numbers available.

The First Player Token (Enlightenment)

  • When the first player token would be passed during Step 4 of the regroup phase (the fate phase), it passes to the player to the left of the first player instead.
  • At the beginning of each dynasty phase, the player with the first player token gains 1 additional fate from the general token pool (to mitigate being forced to act first in the conflict phase).

Enlightenment — Rings

When a player claims a ring, they place it on one of their unbroken provinces, where it remains claimed until that province is broken. The following ring rules apply in the Enlightenment format:

Rings Do Not Return

During step 6 of the fate phase, rings claimed on provinces remain on those provinces instead of returning to the unclaimed ring pool.

Duplicate Rings

A player may never have more than one ring of the same printed element claimed on their provinces at once. If a card effect or framework step would cause a player to claim a duplicate-element ring:

  • If the ring to be claimed is the contested ring, it returns to the attacking player's personal unclaimed ring pool.
  • If the ring to be claimed is on the defending player's province, it moves to the defending player's personal unclaimed ring pool.

Ownership of rings does not need to be tracked.

Placing Fate on Unclaimed Rings in the Fate Phase

Do not place a fate token on each unclaimed ring as normal. Instead, in player order, each player chooses a ring token in a different player's personal unclaimed ring pool and places one fate token from the general pool on it.

Rings and Card Effects

  • A card effect referring to a ring by element refers to any ring with that element; an effect interacting with claimed/unclaimed rings may interact with a ring in any claimed or unclaimed ring pool.
  • When an effect moves a ring between a claimed and an unclaimed ring pool, both pools must belong to the same player. During a conflict, effects can only switch the contested ring with rings in the attacking player's claimed or unclaimed ring pools.

Declaring Conflicts and Claiming Rings

  • The attacking player uses a ring from their personal unclaimed ring pool or, occasionally, from the common unclaimed ring pool.
  • On a win, they resolve the contested ring's effect and place the ring on one of their unbroken provinces; it is not returned at end of round and counts as being in their claimed ring pool for card/game effects.
  • On a defender win, the ring is not claimed and returns to the attacker's personal unclaimed ring pool.
  • A ring cannot be placed on a stronghold province unless each of that player's non-stronghold provinces is broken.
  • Multiple rings can be placed on the same unbroken province.

Taking Rings from Other Players

If a player breaks an opponent's province, they claim any rings that were claimed on it and place them onto their own unbroken provinces (which may be divided among several provinces). If any taken ring has the same printed element as a ring already claimed on the attacking player's provinces, that ring is instead returned to the defending player's personal unclaimed ring pool.

Undefended Conflicts

When a defending player loses an unopposed conflict, they do not lose any honor for the conflict being unopposed.

Ring Availability

If a player has no rings in their personal unclaimed ring pool and none in the common unclaimed ring pool, they may declare a conflict without a contested ring. They still select attacking characters, a province, and a conflict type. With no ring token, the conflict has no element and resolves no ring effect on a win.

The Common Unclaimed Ring Pool

In addition to each player's personal unclaimed ring pool, there is a common unclaimed ring pool any player may use to declare attacks. It is empty at the start of the game; rings enter it via card effects or, more often, by player elimination.

Enlightenment — Treaties (terms)

Players may establish a formal agreement called a treaty. Setting one up requires agreeing on four parameters (in any order; each must be agreed by both involved players before the treaty is finalized):

  1. What Player One promises — something they will do (or not do) during the game. May be specific or vague, but must be an action/decision a player can take and cannot include an exchange of game components (honor, fate, cards, or claimed rings).
  2. What Player Two promises — both players must make a promise (same rules as parameter one) for the treaty to be finalized.
  3. Duration — any length, from "immediate" to "the remainder of the game." If the two promises have different durations, use the longer. When the duration ends, the treaty is either dissolved (both kept their promises) or broken (one did not).
  4. Value — both agree a value to stake, between 1 and 5. If broken, the breaker suffers a penalty based on this value (by default, losing that amount of honor).

Enlightenment — Clarifying Concepts

"In Player Order" and "Pass Consecutively"

  • In player order: the first player acts first, then the player to their left, continuing clockwise.
  • Pass consecutively: a sequence continues until all players pass consecutively (includes action, reaction, and interrupt windows).

"Your Opponent"

  • "Your opponent" refers only to the single opponent participating against you in a conflict. It does not refer to the non-participating player, who cannot fulfill conditions referring to "your opponent."
  • "An opponent" can refer to any of your opponents, even during a conflict.

Eligible Provinces

During a conflict, a province is only an "eligible province" to be attacked if it is controlled by the defending player. The provinces of non-defending players are not eligible.

Participation in a Conflict

  • Only two players control participating characters: one attacking player and one defending player. The other player may take actions to influence the conflict but cannot play or move characters to the conflict on either side (except as indicated by card effects).
  • During the conflict action window, every player (including the non-participating player) may take actions; the window does not end until each player passes consecutively.
  • A conflict cannot resolve without two participating players. If a player is eliminated mid-conflict, the conflict immediately ends with no winner: return the ring to the attacker's unclaimed ring pool (or the common pool, as appropriate) and each participating character controlled by the remaining player goes home bowed.

Enlightenment — Banned List

When deckbuilding, a player may not include any copies of these cards in their decks or provinces:

  • Display of Power (Core Set, 179)
  • Togashi Tadakatsu (Disciples of the Void, 15)
  • Karada District (Imperial Cycle, 4)
  • Ide Tadaji (Imperial Cycle, 29)
  • Jurōjin's Curse (Elemental Cycle, 55)
  • Roving Michibiku (Elemental Cycle, 118)
  • Severed from the Stream (Children of the Empire, 67)

Enlightenment — Ending Treaties

Breaking a Treaty

When a player goes against their promised parameters, the treaty is immediately broken and that player must immediately suffer the consequences (by default, lose honor equal to the staked value; if using treaty cards, the card determines the consequence). After a break, the players are no longer bound by their promises.

Dissolving a Treaty

A treaty dissolves with no penalty if its timeframe has lapsed or both players mutually agree to dissolve it.

Treaty Limits

Each player can form a treaty with any number of other players, but each pair of players may only have one treaty active between them at a time. After a treaty dissolves or breaks, a new one may be formed between those players.

Treaty Cards (Clan War expansion)

  • Treaty cards replace the default honor-staking method. Shuffle them into a treaty deck within reach of all players.
  • When two players agree a treaty, place the top treaty card facedown between them without looking and mark the staked value. When a player breaks the treaty, reveal and resolve the card.
  • A treaty card referring to "the player who did not break this treaty" refers only to the single other player in that treaty.
  • After a treaty is broken (effects resolved) or fulfilled (effects unrevealed), put the card on the bottom of the treaty deck.

Part B — Team Conquest Format (4 players, 2v2)

A four-player team format: two allied players vs. two allied opponents. Each team wins or loses together. Used with the RRG / Learn to Play. The standard game rules apply wherever not explicitly altered, replaced, or negated here.

Team Conquest — Overview, Setup & New Rules

Overview & Setup

  • Four players: two allied players on a team against two allied opponents.
  • At setup, each player on a team sits next to their teammate and across from one opponent. The opponent a player sits across from is the one they exchange honor with during draw-phase honor bids. Otherwise follow standard setup.

Victory and Defeat

The standard win/lose rules are replaced. A team wins a Team Conquest game by either:

  • breaking the stronghold provinces of both opponents, or
  • accumulating a total of 50 honor between both allied players' honor pools.

Teammates and Opponents

  • Both players on a team declare conflicts against opponents to break provinces and strongholds.
  • Characters can be played under a teammate's control to defend that player's provinces.
  • During the draw phase, each player only exchanges honor with the opponent across the table, but either opponent can be attacked during the conflict phase.

The First Player Token

  • Given to a team, not a player; both players on that team are considered the first player.
  • During action windows, each team (not each player) has an action opportunity: one player on the team acts, then a player on the opposing team may act, until both teams pass in sequence.
  • At the end of the regroup phase (fate phase), the token passes to the opposing team.

Playing Characters

A player playing a character from hand or provinces chooses to play it under their own or their teammate's control. Control cannot be exchanged afterward except by card effects. Fate is put on the character from the fate pool of the player playing it, regardless of who controls it.

Passing Fate

Neither player on the team that passes first in the dynasty phase gains fate from the general token pool.

Drawing Cards

  • Each player selects a number on their honor dial as in the RRG. Honor is only exchanged between the two opponents sitting across the table from one another, based on the difference between their bids.
  • If the combined total of two allied players' honor pools is ever 50 or higher, that team wins.

Team Conquest — Favor, Elimination, Unique Cards, Conflicts

Glory Counts and the Imperial Favor

  • The Imperial Favor is controlled by a team. On a glory count, each team sums glory on ready characters they control plus 1 per ring claimed by their team; the higher total wins.
  • During each conflict of the appropriate type, the Imperial Favor contributes +1 skill to the side of the team that controls it.

Player Elimination

Players are not eliminated until their team loses. If a player's stronghold province is broken, they continue to play with these penalties:

  • Treat the printed text box of that player's stronghold as blank (except for Traits).
  • That player cannot bid more than 2 during honor bids.
  • That player cannot reshuffle either of their decks if those decks run out of cards.
  • That player does not need to be attacked by the opposing team before their teammate can be attacked multiple times.
  • If a player reaches 0 honor, their stronghold province is immediately broken.
  • Broken stronghold provinces are immune to all card effects.

Unique Cards (team rules, replacing the standard unique rules)

  • A player cannot take control of or bring into play a unique card if any player on their team already controls or owns another in-play card with the same title or printed title.
  • A player cannot bring into play a unique card owned by either opponent if one of those opponents already controls an in-play card with the same title or printed title.
  • As a player action during the dynasty phase, a player may discard a copy (by title) of a unique character from hand or provinces to place 1 fate on an in-play copy controlled by any player on their team.
  • While a team controls more than one copy of a unique stronghold, province, or holding, treat each copy as blank with a strength (or bonus strength) of 0.

Declaring Conflicts

  • A team (not a player) declares a conflict or passes. Each team can declare only one military and one political conflict, and each must be declared against a province controlled by a different opponent. Opportunities alternate between teams, starting with the first players.
  • The declaring team makes the standard selections (attacking characters, conflict type, an eligible province, and the contested ring). Both players on a team may declare attackers; those characters participate on the same side together.
  • When an attacking team would gain fate from the contested ring, they choose which attacking player gains that fate.
  • Additional conflict opportunities granted by card effects go to that player's team. Any conflicts beyond the first two may be declared against any province. A team must attack a province controlled by each opponent before attacking a player's provinces more than once, unless a player's stronghold province is broken.
  • When a player's province is attacked, they may declare any number of their characters as defenders, and their teammate may also declare up to 1 character as a defender. Both count as defending players.
  • Conflicts cannot be declared against a stronghold province unless that player controls at least 1 broken non-stronghold province AND that player's team controls at least 3 total broken provinces.

Resolving Conflicts

  • Players win or lose as a team; "when a player wins/loses a conflict" abilities trigger for both players on the team.
  • If the defending team controls no defending characters, the conflict is unopposed and each player on that team loses 1 honor. If any player on the defending team controls a defending character, no one loses honor for unopposed.
  • On a win, the attacking team may resolve the contested ring's effect as normal. The Air and Earth ring effects may only be resolved against the opponent whose province is being attacked, and only one player on the attacking team (not both) may receive the ring's effect.

Team Conquest — FAQ, Additional Players & Clarifications

Frequently Asked Questions

Hida Kisada (Core Set, 37): Kisada cancels the first Action ability triggered by a player on the opposing team during each conflict, but cancels a total of only one ability each conflict. His controller does not select which opponent the ability applies to — either opponent could qualify, and it is mandatory.

Additional Players (more than 4)

  • Honor Victory: with six or more total players, a team wins when its combined honor equals 25 per player on the team (3 players → 75 honor, 4 players → 100 honor, etc.).
  • Stronghold Victory: a team loses when each player's stronghold province is broken. A team's stronghold provinces become eligible to be attacked when the team collectively controls X broken provinces, where X = 1 + the number of players on that team (3 players → 4 broken, 4 players → 5 broken, etc.).

"In Player Order" and "Pass Consecutively"

  • In player order: the first players act as a team (selecting one of their players), then the opposing team acts (selecting one of theirs), then the other player on the first-player team, then the final player.
  • Pass consecutively: continues until both teams pass consecutively (a team passes instead of a player) — includes action, reaction, and interrupt windows.

"Your Opponent"

"Your opponent" / "an opponent" refers to either player on the opposing team, chosen when resolving the card ability.

Duels

Both characters chosen for a duel must be controlled by players on opposing teams. All other duel parameters from the RRG still apply.

Claimed and Unclaimed Rings

  • A single unclaimed ring pool holds the five rings used to declare conflicts; a team selects one when declaring a conflict, as in the standard game. (Unlike the Enlightenment format, Team Conquest uses one shared unclaimed ring pool, not personal pools.)
  • Each team has a shared claimed ring pool, counting as the claimed ring pool of both players on that team for card/game effects.

Participation in a Conflict

  • All four players may control participating characters. Characters controlled by players on the same team participate on the same side and contribute to the same total. Any character may be moved/put/played into the conflict on the side of its controller's team. Any effect referencing "a player's side of the conflict" references that player's team's side instead.
  • During the conflict action window, each team has an action opportunity; the window does not end until each team passes consecutively.

Eligible Provinces

A province is only an "eligible province" to be attacked if it is controlled by the defending player. The provinces of non-defending players are not eligible.

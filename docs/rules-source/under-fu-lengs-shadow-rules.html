Overview

Under Fu Leng's Shadow is an expansion for Legend of the Five Rings: The Card Game. It introduces new cards, rules, and game components to support playing the game against the Shadowlands faction. The faction is designed to be played as a cooperative experience in which 1–4 players work together to withstand waves of attacks. Alternatively, it can be played as a player-driven challenge deck where one player commands the Shadowlands against a team of 2–4 opponents.

The expansion includes 138 non-Shadowlands cards. These are highlighted in the new formats but can also be used in standard Legend of the Five Rings play.

Two Formats supported

The rules accommodate both formats and assume players already know the normal rules of the game:

  • Stronghold format — uses 5 provinces and a stronghold.
  • Skirmish format — uses 3 provinces (no stronghold).

The rulebook recommends new players learn the skirmish format before the stronghold format. (Learn-to-Play documents for both are at www.L5R.com.)

Components (rules-relevant)

The component list is for identification only; no rules. Counts retained for reference (they bound deck-construction): 3 Shadowlands Warlord cards (double-sided), 10 Shadowlands Province cards, 2 Stronghold cards, 60 Shadowlands Dynasty cards, 60 Shadowlands Conflict cards, 64 (non-Shadowlands) Dynasty cards, 72 (non-Shadowlands) Conflict cards, and 20 double-sided Status tokens (Tainted).

Shadowlands Faction Rules

The Shadowlands faction changes how the game is played: it exists as an external threat that the clans of Rokugan must defeat together.

With the exception of the rules listed below, the Shadowlands faction is treated as a clan just like the seven clans currently in the game. It is identified by the [shadowlands] clan symbol in text and by its unique card frame. The following rules apply to the Shadowlands faction in all game modes.

Team Play

  • The Shadowlands is not meant to be faced by a single opponent. Players who oppose the Shadowlands do so as a team, and the Shadowlands faction operates as a team of one.
  • Even when playing a single-player cooperative game against a Shadowlands deck, all of the rules for team play must be observed.
  • Throughout the rulebook, both the team of players opposing the Shadowlands and the Shadowlands itself are referred to as teams, regardless of the number of players on that team.

Full team rules: Appendix III (p. 24).

Winning the Game (vs. the Shadowlands)

When playing with or against the Shadowlands, only use the following win conditions (the normal honor-total and standard victory conditions do not apply to the Shadowlands side — see Honor below):

  • If the Shadowlands dynasty deck has 0 cards in it at the end of any game round, the Shadowlands deck (or player) loses the game.
  • If all of the Shadowlands provinces are broken, the Shadowlands deck (or player) immediately loses the game.
  • If each player's stronghold province is broken (stronghold format), or all non-Shadowlands provinces are broken (skirmish format), the Shadowlands deck (or player) immediately wins the game.

Honor

  • The Shadowlands faction has no clan honor, and a team of opponents cannot win by accumulating a sufficient honor total.
  • If an effect would cause the Shadowlands deck (or player) to gain or lose honor, it is ignored. Honor costs are always considered to be paid by the Shadowlands.
  • If a player would be instructed to take honor from / give honor to their Shadowlands opponent (or vice-versa), that player gains honor from or loses honor to the general token pool instead.

Surviving the Shadowlands

Because the Shadowlands dynasty deck is also the timer leading toward its opponents' victory condition, card effects cannot discard cards from the Shadowlands dynasty deck.

Banned Cards (when playing against the Shadowlands)

The following cards are banned; players cannot include them in their decks:

  • Trading on the Sand Road (Warriors of the Wind, 28)
  • Haughty Magistrate (Imperial Cycle, 69)
  • Waning Hostilities (Imperial Cycle, 100)
  • Hige's Sermon (Clan War, 92)
  • A Season of War (Dominion Cycle, 14)

Warlords

  • Each Shadowlands deck is built around a unique warlord who defines how the game is played from round to round.
  • Warlord cards are double-sided: one side for cooperative play and one for challenge play. Use the side matching the game mode; the mode is indicated by a tag at the bottom of the card's text box.
  • The warlord starts the game out of play, visible between the two Shadowlands decks.
  • Instead of a cost, the warlord has a fate value in its upper-left corner that indicates how much fate the Shadowlands collects each round.
  • Each warlord has a powerful ability that can be triggered once per round, even while the warlord is out of play.
  • The warlord can be brought into play by card effects, at which point it functions like any other character. When it would leave play, it returns to its out-of-play zone (where its ability can continue to be used and from which it can be brought back into play later).

Example warlord shown on this page (card data, not a rule): Akuma no Oni — "Champion. Corrupted. No [void] attachments." with a once-per-round Reaction, tagged Cooperative. Full warlord card text belongs in the card catalog.

Per-Player Icon

  • The per-player icon [per-player] is used on Shadowlands warlord and province cards to scale a value by the number of players.
  • When a number is followed by the [per-player] symbol, multiply that number by the number of non-Shadowlands players in the game to determine that value.
  • Players will not be eliminated during the game.

Tainted Status and Corrupted Keyword

  • Many Shadowlands card effects can taint a character or province. To indicate a card is tainted, place a tainted status token on it.
  • A tainted card is stronger when tainted, but carries an honor cost in order to attack or defend.
  • Because the Shadowlands is always considered to pay honor costs, the cost to use a tainted card is ignored by the Shadowlands faction.
  • Many Shadowlands characters have the corrupted keyword, which means they enter play already tainted.

Full rules on tainted status and the corrupted keyword: Appendix I (p. 21).

Shadowlands Ring Effects

When a Shadowlands player would resolve a ring effect, they use a different set of ring effects than their opponents (the standard ring effects are corrupted by Jigoku):

  • Air: Each opponent loses 1 honor.
  • Earth: Each opponent chooses 1 faceup card in their provinces and discards it, then discards 1 random card from their hand.
  • Fire: Each opponent chooses and taints a character they control.
  • Water: Each opponent chooses and bows a character in their home.
  • Void: Each opponent removes 1 fate from a character they control.

Assembling Shadowlands Decks

  • Each Shadowlands deck is built around its warlord and contains two 40-card decks (dynasty + conflict) and five provinces.
  • The Shadowlands dynasty and conflict cards are organized into twelve encounter groups of ten cards each, identified by a number on the bottom of each card. There are six dynasty encounter groups and six conflict encounter groups.
  • To build a Shadowlands deck: select the warlord and each of that warlord's provinces (listed by title below). Then select the four 10-card encounter groups listed for the dynasty deck and shuffle them together, and the four listed for the conflict deck and shuffle them together. This creates a 40-card dynasty deck and a 40-card conflict deck. The two decks, five provinces, and warlord together form the Shadowlands deck.
  • For a first game against the Shadowlands, the rulebook recommends playing against Akuma no Oni.

Cooperative Mode

In cooperative mode, 1–4 players work together to defeat an army of minions besieging their provinces. The standard game rules apply wherever they are not explicitly altered, replaced, or negated by this document.

The Shadowlands Deck

  • A team of players opposes a Shadowlands deck not run by any individual player (referred to as the "Shadowlands deck").
  • The players are one team; the Shadowlands deck is the opposing team for the purposes of the team rules (Appendix III, p. 24).

Setup

  • The Shadowlands deck is composed of a 40-card dynasty deck, a 40-card conflict deck, five provinces, and a warlord. These are laid out in a pair of rows in front of the players, with the warlord and the two decks behind the five provinces.
  • The Shadowlands in-play home area is in front of its provinces (closer to the players).
  • All Shadowlands provinces begin the game facedown and in a random order.
  • If playing the skirmish format, five province tokens are used instead of province cards (per the skirmish-format rulebook).
  • After setting up the Shadowlands deck, the players shuffle their own decks and set up as normal (filling provinces, drawing opening hands).
  • The Shadowlands deck has no hand of cards. Each of its provinces starts the game empty and tainted. The Shadowlands deck always starts with the first player token.

Key Concepts (Cooperative Mode)

Making Choices

  • When a card ability requires the Shadowlands deck to make a choice or selection, the players decide together how it is made.
  • Exception — Sacrifice: when the Shadowlands deck is instructed to sacrifice a card in play, it always sacrifices the card with the lowest printed fate cost among all possible options.

"You" and the Shadowlands Player

  • On Shadowlands cards, "you" refers to the Shadowlands deck and "your opponent(s)" refers to the player(s) opposing it.
  • The Shadowlands deck is considered to be a player for the purposes of resolving card abilities, even though no player operates it.

Abilities Are Not Optional

  • The Shadowlands deck always triggers its abilities whenever possible, and always pays as many optional costs as it can.
  • As in the standard game, each action, reaction, and interrupt ability can only be triggered once each round.

Reinforcement Number

  • Each Shadowlands character has a reinforcement number located adjacent to its fate cost — how many rounds that character stays in play in cooperative mode.
  • When the Shadowlands deck plays a character, place fate on it from the general token pool equal to its reinforcement number.
  • If a card effect puts a Shadowlands character into play from its provinces or discard pile, ignore the reinforcement number — it receives no fate beyond what that effect instructs.

Shadow Elements and Shadow Numbers

  • Certain Shadowlands decisions are made automatically by referencing the shadow element or shadow number printed on each Shadowlands conflict card.
  • Shadow numbers are randomizers used to determine how the Shadowlands deck bids during the draw phase and during duels, and to select which provinces are attacked when it declares a conflict.
  • Shadow elements are randomizers used to select the contested ring when the Shadowlands player declares a conflict.

The shadow-element symbols are defined in Appendix II (card anatomy, pp. 22–23); their exact mapping to the five rings is cross-checked there. (The page-13 example names a shadow element "Madness.")

Cooperative — Dynasty Phase

The cooperative game uses the same phase sequence as any other format. Both teams alternate taking dynasty actions, starting with the team holding the first player token. Once a team passes, no player on that team may take actions or play cards for the rest of the phase.

Shadowlands Provinces (refill)

  • At the start of each dynasty phase, fill each Shadowlands province with one faceup card (1–2 players) or two faceup cards (3–4 players) from the Shadowlands dynasty deck — even if that province already has one or more cards in it.
  • Shadowlands provinces are never refilled when empty; they only receive new cards from the Shadowlands deck at the start of the dynasty phase (or through card effects).

Shadowlands Fate Collection

  • After filling each province, the Shadowlands deck collects 5 fate per player, as indicated by the warlord.
  • After collecting fate, the Shadowlands deck triggers the warlord's ability, even if it is out of play.

Shadowlands Dynasty Actions

  • On its dynasty action, the Shadowlands deck attempts to play a character from its provinces. If it cannot, it passes and takes no more actions for the rest of the round.
  • The character played is the one in the leftmost Shadowlands province whose fate cost can be paid from the Shadowlands deck's fate pool. If the leftmost province contains two or more eligible characters, the Shadowlands deck always plays the most expensive eligible option first; on a tie, the players choose.
  • After playing the leftmost character, it attempts to play the next leftmost character (even if in the same province), moving right and skipping any character it cannot pay for.

Cooperative — Playing Characters & Draw Phase

Playing a Shadowlands character

  • Spend the appropriate fate from the Shadowlands deck's fate pool and place the character in the Shadowlands deck's home area.
  • It enters play with fate on it equal to its reinforcement number, taken from the general pool.

Passing First

The team that passes first in the dynasty phase does not gain any fate, regardless of game format.

Draw Phase

Each player bids with their honor dial to draw cards, while the Shadowlands deck counter-bids using the top card of its conflict deck to gain fate. Each player compares their honor bid to that conflict card's shadow number and gains or loses honor equal to the difference.

Honor Bids

  • Each player selects a number on their honor dial (1–5 stronghold, 1–3 skirmish) for how many cards to draw.
  • Reveal dials, then discard the top card of the Shadowlands conflict deck.
  • Each player whose bid is higher than the discarded card's shadow number loses honor equal to the difference; each player whose bid is lower gains honor equal to the difference.
  • Each player draws cards equal to their honor bid.
  • The Shadowlands deck gains fate equal to the discarded card's shadow number × the number of players.

Cooperative — Conflict Phase

Both teams alternate declaring conflicts against each other's provinces, starting with the team holding the first player token.

  • Stronghold format: the team of players has two conflict opportunities (one military, one political), while the Shadowlands deck can continue declaring conflicts until it runs out of ready characters and/or unclaimed rings to attack with.
  • Skirmish format: each team has only one conflict opportunity.

Shadowlands Waves

  • Shadowlands characters are organized into waves: a wave contains each ready character with a certain amount of fate on it.
  • When a conflict is declared, only the wave with the fewest fate attacks or defends in that conflict (the "next wave").
  • If any of those characters cannot participate in a conflict together, then each eligible character in that wave participates, and the remaining characters form a new wave for the next conflict.

Shadowlands Conflict Declaration

Four decisions are made: three automatically, and the fourth (conflict type) by the defending players.

  • Attacking characters (auto): each eligible character in the next wave is declared as an attacker.
  • Contested ring (auto): discard the top card of the Shadowlands conflict deck; its shadow element indicates the contested ring's element. If the shadow element does not match a ring in the unclaimed ring pool, continue discarding until a card whose shadow element matches an unclaimed ring is found.
  • Attacked provinces (auto): the same discarded card's shadow number selects the position. Each player's leftmost non-stronghold province is position #1, increasing to #4 at the rightmost. The conflict is declared against the non-stronghold province in the matching position; if that province is broken, it is declared against that player's stronghold instead.
    • Skirmish format: the Shadowlands deck's single attack each round is instead declared against each player's leftmost unbroken province.

Cooperative — Multi-province Attacks & Conflict Type

  • Each Shadowlands conflict is declared against one province per player. With two or more players this becomes a conflict against multiple provinces at once. The conflict is considered to be at each of those provinces, but the players defend all of them with a single group of defenders.
  • Conflict type (the fourth decision): the players choose the type, since they are deciding how to defend. If any attacking character has a dash skill value, or otherwise cannot participate in a conflict of a certain type, then the conflict cannot be of that type — the attack must still be legal.

Per the page-13 example, the players will choose the conflict type that is most advantageous to them (e.g. the type that gives the attacking characters the least applicable skill).

Cooperative — Dash Eligibility & Conflict Actions

Dash Skill Values and Participation Eligibility

  • A character with a dash skill value (or with Pacifism / Stolen Breath attached) cannot participate in a conflict of the corresponding type. This may force an attack to be a particular type and may prevent that character from defending against that type. Cards such as Peacemaker's Blade can prevent Shadowlands characters from being declared as attackers.
  • If every Shadowlands character in the next wave of attackers cannot be declared as an attacker, that wave is skipped over and the subsequent next wave attacks instead.
  • If every Shadowlands character in the next wave of defenders cannot be declared as a defender, that wave is not skipped — the Shadowlands deck declares no defenders.
  • If the Shadowlands deck would attack with a wave where some characters cannot participate in a military conflict and others cannot participate in a political conflict, the players select the type and the character(s) unable to participate do not attack; they form a new wave for the next conflict.

Conflict Actions

  • During a conflict, the team of players and the Shadowlands deck alternate action opportunities. The Shadowlands deck always takes the first action in each conflict, regardless of which team is defending.
  • On a Shadowlands action opportunity, reveal the top card of the conflict deck. If it can be played (enough fate, eligible targets, and the effect can change the game state), it is played. All attachments played this way must go on a participating character or an attacked province. If the revealed card cannot be played, discard it (the deck has "passed" its action).
  • Once the team of players passes, the Shadowlands deck immediately passes and the conflict resolves.

Running out of Cards

  • If the top card of the Shadowlands conflict deck is needed (for an honor bid, conflict declaration, or conflict action) and the conflict deck is empty, immediately reshuffle the conflict discard pile into a new conflict deck. The Shadowlands deck does not lose honor for running out of cards.
  • If the Shadowlands dynasty deck has no cards in it at the end of any game round, the players win the game.

Cooperative — Resolving a Conflict

Resolve following the normal conflict-resolution rules, with these clarifications for the team:

  • The players win or lose the conflict as a team, regardless of who controls participating characters. "When a player wins/loses a conflict" abilities trigger for each player on the team, as each counts as having won (or lost) the conflict.
  • If the players control no defending characters and are playing the stronghold format when the Shadowlands deck wins as the attacker, each player loses 1 honor for the unopposed conflict.
  • A Shadowlands conflict declared against multiple provinces can break multiple provinces with a single attack. Each player determines whether their attacked province is broken independently of the others.
  • The team that wins claims the contested ring into their shared claimed ring pool. For card and game effects, a team's claimed ring pool counts as the claimed ring pool of each player on that team.

When a Shadowlands province is broken

  • Discard each card in that province along with five cards from the top of the Shadowlands dynasty deck.
  • A broken Shadowlands province cannot be attacked, but does receive cards during the dynasty phase (which can then be played).
  • If all five Shadowlands provinces are broken, the players immediately win the game.

Cooperative — Card Abilities & Imperial Favor

Shadowlands Card Abilities (by card type)

Abilities on non-unique Shadowlands cards are organized by card type:

  • Warlord: ability triggers each round during the dynasty phase, after the Shadowlands deck collects fate.
  • Event: an action ability that can be played during a conflict (when Shadowlands conflict cards are played).
  • Attachment (non-unique): a reaction ability that triggers after it is played; afterward the attachment provides only skill bonuses.
  • Character (non-unique): a reaction ability that triggers when that character wins a conflict (on attack or defense). Each unique Shadowlands character has a reaction or interrupt ability with a triggering condition unique to it.
  • Province card (and each attachment that attaches to a province): an ability that triggers when a conflict is declared against that province.
  • Holding: an ability that triggers at the end of the conflict phase.
  • As in the standard game, each action/reaction/interrupt ability can only be triggered once each round unless the card specifies otherwise.

Glory Counts and the Imperial Favor

  • The Imperial Favor can only be controlled by the team of players, and is shared by the team rather than any individual. If any player on the team discards it via a card ability, it is lost by the entire team. The Shadowlands deck cannot have the Imperial Favor.
  • On a glory count, each team adds together the total glory on ready characters they control plus one for each ring claimed by their team; compare the two totals.
  • If the team of players wins the glory count, it claims the Imperial Favor as normal. If the Shadowlands deck wins the glory count, discard the Imperial Favor from play.

Cooperative — Fate Phase & Difficulty Options

Fate Phase

  • Resolves as normal: discard each character with no fate, remove one fate from each other character. In the stronghold format, place one fate on each unclaimed ring and discard each card from each broken province.
  • Each team returns its claimed rings to the unclaimed ring pool, and the first player token is given to the opposing team.
  • The end of the fate phase ends the game round. Proceed to the next round and continue until one team has won.

For an Additional Challenge (optional difficulty rules)

One or more may be used to increase difficulty (use all five for a "nightmarish" challenge):

  • Cunning — When the Shadowlands deck would take an action during a conflict in which it is attacking, that action opportunity is passed if the Shadowlands deck is currently winning and breaking the province.
  • Dangerous — When the Shadowlands deck declares a conflict, the conflict type is determined by the attacking characters' skills: total their military and political skills separately. The conflict is always declared military unless their total political skill exceeds their total military skill. (If any character cannot participate in a conflict of a particular type, it cannot be declared of that type.)
  • Greedy — When the Shadowlands deck declares a conflict, if any rings have fate on them, it can only declare using a ring with the most fate on it. Discard conflict cards until one whose shadow element matches an unclaimed ring with the most fate, and use that card to determine the conflict's type and location.
  • Relentless — The Shadowlands deck collects 1 additional fate per player each round.
  • Unpredictable — When the Shadowlands deck is instructed to make a choice or selection while resolving card abilities, that choice is made randomly among all available options; the players do not choose for the Shadowlands deck.

Challenge Mode

In challenge mode, one player plays as the Shadowlands (the "Shadowlands player") and takes on a team of 2–4 opponents. The standard game rules apply wherever they are not explicitly altered, replaced, or negated by this document.

The Shadowlands Team

A single player playing as the Shadowlands opposes a team of 2–4 opponents. Each side is referred to as a "team of players" even though the Shadowlands player is a team of one.

Setup

  • The Shadowlands player has a 40-card dynasty deck, a 40-card conflict deck, five provinces, and a warlord, laid out with the warlord and two decks behind the five provinces (closest to the Shadowlands player). The Shadowlands in-play "home" area is in front of the provinces (closer to the opposing team).
  • All Shadowlands provinces begin the game facedown and in a random order. (Skirmish format: province tokens instead of province cards.)
  • The Shadowlands player starts with a hand of zero cards and an honor dial. Each of their provinces starts the game empty and tainted.
  • The opposing team sets up as normal (filling provinces, drawing opening hands).
  • Randomly determine which team starts with the first player token.

Key difference from cooperative mode: the Shadowlands is a real player who draws a hand and chooses fate placement; many decisions that are automatic in cooperative mode are made by the Shadowlands player here.

Challenge — Dynasty & Draw Phases

Same phase sequence as any other format. Both teams alternate dynasty actions, starting with the team holding the first player token; once a team passes, no player on it may act for the rest of the phase.

Shadowlands Provinces (refill)

  • At the start of each dynasty phase, fill each Shadowlands province with one faceup card from the Shadowlands dynasty deck — even if that province already has one or more cards in it. (Note: in challenge mode this is always one card, not scaled by player count.)
  • Shadowlands provinces are never refilled when empty; they only receive new cards at the start of the dynasty phase (or through card effects).

Shadowlands Fate Collection and Card Draw

  • After filling provinces, the Shadowlands player collects 5 fate per opponent, as indicated by the warlord.
  • After collecting fate, the Shadowlands player draws two cards per opponent from their conflict deck into their hand.

Dynasty Actions

When the Shadowlands player plays a character from their provinces, they may place any amount of fate on it from their fate pool as in the standard rules, ignoring the character's reinforcement number.

Passing First

The team that passes first in the dynasty phase does not gain any fate, regardless of format.

Draw Phase

  • The Shadowlands player uses an honor dial to draw cards as in the standard game.
  • Each player bids with their honor dial as normal. Each non-Shadowlands player compares their bid to the Shadowlands player's honor bid and gains or loses honor equal to the difference.
  • The Shadowlands player has no honor value and does not gain or lose honor for bidding high or low, although they can cause opponents to gain or lose honor accordingly.

Challenge — Conflict & Fate Phases

Conflict Phase

  • Both teams alternate declaring conflicts, starting with the team holding the first player token.
  • Stronghold format: the non-Shadowlands team has two conflict opportunities (one military, one political) each round; the Shadowlands player has one conflict opportunity per opponent each round and must alternate between declaring military and political conflicts.
  • Skirmish format: each team has one conflict opportunity each round.
  • Unlike cooperative mode, the Shadowlands player attacks a single province, controlled by a single player, with each conflict. The controller of the attacked province may declare one or more defenders if they choose, and each other player on their team may declare up to one defender.
  • The Shadowlands player cannot declare more than one conflict against the same opponent until they have declared a conflict against a province controlled by each opponent whose stronghold province is unbroken.
  • Shadowlands conflicts cannot be declared against an opponent's stronghold province unless that opponent controls at least one broken non-stronghold province AND that player's team controls at least X total broken provinces, where X = the number of players in the game (including the Shadowlands player).

Glory Counts and the Imperial Favor

  • The Imperial Favor can only be controlled by the team of non-Shadowlands players, shared by the team. The Shadowlands player cannot have the Imperial Favor.
  • On a glory count, each team sums glory on ready characters they control plus one per ring claimed by their team; the higher total wins. If the non-Shadowlands team wins, it claims the Favor as normal. If the Shadowlands player wins, discard the Imperial Favor from play.

Fate Phase

  • Resolves as normal: discard characters with no fate, remove one fate from each other character. Stronghold format: place one fate on each unclaimed ring and discard each card from each broken province.
  • Each team returns claimed rings to the unclaimed ring pool; the first player token passes to the opposing team. The round then ends; continue until one team has won.

Appendix I: New Mechanics

This expansion introduces a new tainted status token and two new keywords (corrupted, support). These mechanics can be used in any format.

Tainted Status

  • When a card ability or ring effect would taint a character, place a tainted status token on it. A tainted character cannot be tainted again.
  • Tainted character: gets +2 military and +2 political. As an additional cost to declare a tainted character as an attacker or defender in a conflict, its controller must lose 1 honor.
  • Tainted province: gets +2 strength. As an additional cost to declare any number of defenders in a conflict against a tainted province, its controller must lose 1 honor.
  • Once a card is tainted, the tainted status cannot be removed unless a card ability discards (or moves) its status token. If a tainted province is turned faceup or facedown, do not discard its tainted status token.
  • A character's tainted status has no bearing on its personal honor; a tainted character can be honored or dishonored the same as an untainted character.

Recall (p. 5): because the Shadowlands always pays honor costs, the honor cost to use a tainted card is ignored by the Shadowlands faction.

Corrupted Keyword

  • A character with the corrupted keyword enters play already tainted.
  • Abilities cannot be triggered from a corrupted character receiving the tainted status token from this keyword (it enters play already with that status — there is no taint event to react to).

Support Keyword

  • The support keyword lets multiple players pay the fate cost of a card together. When a player plays a card with support, another player may choose to help pay its fate cost.
  • The playing player may solicit other players to contribute, or another player may offer to contribute fate.
  • No player is ever obligated to pay for a support card, and the playing player is never obligated to accept fate from another player.
  • If a player attempts to play a support card they cannot fully pay for and no one supports them, it remains unplayed in their hand and they pay none of the card's costs.

Appendix II: Shadowlands Card Anatomy

New fields appearing on Shadowlands cards (the field definitions; per-card values are catalog data):

  1. Fate Value (warlord, upper-left) — the amount of fate this warlord provides each dynasty phase.
  2. Game Mode Indicator (warlord, bottom of text box) — which game mode that version of the warlord is used in (e.g. Cooperative / Challenge).
  3. Per-Player Icon — the preceding number is multiplied by the number of non-Shadowlands players. Appears on warlord and province cards.
  4. Reinforcement Number (character, near fate cost) — the amount of fate placed on this character from the token pool when it is played from a province in cooperative mode.
  5. Encounter Group (bottom of card) — cards are included in Shadowlands decks by group rather than individually. There are six dynasty encounter groups and six conflict encounter groups.
  6. Shadow Number — used to determine Shadowlands honor bids and conflict declaration in cooperative mode. Appears on Shadowlands conflict-deck cards.
  7. Shadow Element — used to determine Shadowlands conflict declaration (the contested ring) in cooperative mode. Appears on Shadowlands conflict-deck cards.

Appendix II (cont.): Field placement by card type

Per the anatomy diagrams, the new fields appear on the following card types:

  • Warlord: Fate Value (1), Per-Player Icon (3), Game Mode Indicator (2).
  • Province: Per-Player Icon (3).
  • Character: Reinforcement Number (4), Encounter Group (5).
  • Attachment (conflict deck): Encounter Group (5), Shadow Number (6), Shadow Element (7).
  • Holding (dynasty deck): Encounter Group (5).
  • Event (conflict deck): Encounter Group (5), Shadow Number (6), Shadow Element (7).

The shadow number and shadow element appear on Shadowlands conflict-deck cards (attachments and events) — these are the cards drawn/discarded as randomizers for honor bids and conflict declaration. The exact set of shadow-element symbols and their mapping to the five rings is per-card catalog data (the page-13 example names one shadow element "Madness"); confirm against the printed cards when implementing.
